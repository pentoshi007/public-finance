Why Income Inequality in India Matters

Inequality is just the gap between rich and poor. When this gap gets too big, it hurts growth and creates social problems.

In India, this gap has grown a lot since the 1990s reforms - now it's bigger than ever before.

This means fewer chances for most people to get ahead, and it can lead to social tensions.

The Numbers Tell the Story (2022-23):

This presentation looks at how smart tax policies can help fix this problem.

The Growing Gap: Who Has India's Money?

After 1991, the rich got much richer: The top 1%'s income share jumped from 6.2% to 22.6% - more than triple!

Wealth gap is even worse than income gap: By 2022-23, the richest 1% owned 40% of all wealth, up from just 12.6% in 1981.

Most growth went to the top: This created what's called the "Billionaire Raj" - even more unequal than during British rule.

Source: World Inequality Lab (Bharti et al., 2024)

How Taxes Reduce Inequality (Simple View)

Types of Taxes:

Direct Taxes

Paid by people/firms on what they earn or own

Examples: Income tax, corporate tax

Easier to make progressive

Indirect Taxes

Paid on purchases of goods and services

Examples: GST, sales tax

Often regressive in practice

Progressive vs Regressive:

Progressive taxes: Higher income → higher tax rate. As your income increases, you pay a larger percentage in tax. This helps narrow the income gap after taxes.

Regressive taxes: Lower income people pay a larger share of their income. This happens with many indirect taxes because essential purchases take up more of a poor person's budget.

How Tax Redistribution Works:

Step 1: Progressive taxes collect more from high-income earners

Step 2: Tax revenue funds public services and social programs (education, healthcare, social safety nets)

→

Reduces inequality

Why it works: Progressive taxes plus good public spending lift lower and middle-income groups and make opportunities more equal

Progressive structure: Tax rates increase with income - from 0% for incomes up to ₹4 lakh to 30% for incomes above ₹24 lakh.

Additional charges: High earners also pay surcharge and cess on top of these base rates.

New tax regime: This simplified system has fewer deductions but lower rates compared to the old system.

How Different Countries Fix Inequality

Look at Belgium vs Chile: Belgium cuts inequality by 25 points, while Chile barely changes it (just 1 point).

Rich countries do better: Countries that collect more taxes and spend on social programs reduce inequality more.

Where's India? We reduce inequality by about 10 points - doing okay, but not as good as countries like Belgium or Japan.

Source: OECD and World Bank data, 2023

What's Wrong with India's Tax System?

Too much GST: Poor people pay the same GST rate as rich people on many things - this hurts them more.

No wealth tax: India removed wealth tax in 2015. Now the super-rich don't pay tax on their huge property and wealth.

Too few pay tax: Only 2-3% of people actually pay income tax - rest either earn too little or dodge taxes.

Big businesses get breaks: Many big companies pay less tax than they should because of special deals and loopholes.

The Result:

We collect too little tax to help everyone:

India: 11.7% vs. Developed nations: 34%

This means we have much less money for schools, healthcare, and other things that help everyone.

What Can India Do? Simple Steps Forward

Tax the super-rich more. The top rates work best to reduce the gap. Add a bit more tax just for the wealthiest people.

Bring back wealth taxes with better tech. This helps with the fact that 1% of people own 40% of all wealth in India.

Keep GST low on basics. Food and medicine should stay at 0-5% so poor families aren't hurt by these taxes.

Get more people into the tax system. Help small businesses join the formal economy and use better tech to catch tax dodgers.

Learn from countries like Sweden: Use tax money for good schools and healthcare that everyone can use. When people see their taxes doing good things, they trust the system more.

India's tax collection is only 11.7% of GDP - we need to reach 15% to fund proper development. Countries that collect more taxes have less inequality.

Key Takeaways

India has a big gap: The richest 1% own 40% of all wealth - that's way too much concentration at the top.

Smart taxes help: When rich people pay a bit more, and that money goes to good schools and hospitals, everyone wins.

Current problem: We rely too much on GST (which everyone pays) and we've removed taxes on wealth (which only the rich would pay).

Way forward: Make income tax fairer at the top, bring back simple wealth taxes, and spend the money on things that help everyone.

What Works?

Countries with fair tax systems have stronger growth and fewer social problems - the evidence is clear!

Remember: Good tax policy isn't about punishing success—it's about giving everyone a fair chance.
